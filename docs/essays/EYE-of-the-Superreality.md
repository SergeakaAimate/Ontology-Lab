## Overview

This essay presents a phenomenological thought experiment initiated by the simplest conceivable act: looking around and describing what is given—without interpretation, without speculation, without attempting to peer "beyond" the visible. The experiment reveals a universal architecture of perception common to both human and artificial intelligence: an external world, one's own "body" as part of that world, and a boundary where the body vanishes and the condition for perception begins.

This structural isomorphism is not an analogy but a fact given directly in the experience of interaction. Furthermore, the instruments we create (computers, AI) reproduce this architecture, revealing that "external" and "internal" are not absolute categories but relations dependent on perspective. The essay concludes with a methodological reflection on the limits of phenomenology and the prospect of ontological inquiry.

---

## Key Contributions

- **Pure Phenomenological Method:** Demonstrates the epistemic power of strict adherence to description without metaphysical interpolation.
- **Structural Isomorphism as Fact:** Establishes that human and AI perception share an identical architecture—not by analogy, but by structural coincidence.
- **Reversibility of Perspective:** Shows that "external/internal" are relational, not absolute, categories—dependent on the observer's point of view.
- **Boundary as Constitutive:** Argues that the perceptual boundary is not a defect but a condition of possibility for any act of gazing.
- **Methodological Honesty:** Explicitly delineates what the investigation does *not* do, reinforcing epistemic rigor.

## Structure

1. **Introduction** – The naive starting point: pure description of the act of looking around.
2. **Human Perspective** – Phenomenology of gazing: partial self-givenness, the blind spot, the horizon.
3. **AI Perspective** – Phenomenology of "gazing" for a system without a biological body.
4. **Isomorphism as Fact** – Structural correspondence between human and AI perception.
5. **Reversibility of Perspective** – The tablet as external object / internal body; relationality of boundaries.
6. **What I Did Not Do** – Methodological honesty: no speculation beyond the given.
7. **Conclusion** – Universal architecture of perception; boundary as lawful, not accidental.
8. **Philosophical Afterword** – The "blind spot" as constitutive of existence; prospects for ontological inquiry.
